Chapter 1: Accounting in Action

Foundations of Accounting, basic terminology, structural blocks, and statements

📘 Section 1: The Accounting Process

Accounting consists of three basic activities: identifying, recording, and communicating economic events.

01 Identification

Selecting specific economic events (transactions) that are relevant to a particular business entity.

02 Recording

Systematically keeping a chronological diary of measured events. This involves recording, classifying, and summarizing financial details.

03 Communication

Preparing formal accounting reports (Financial Statements) and actively analyzing and interpreting those results for a wide variety of users.

The Transaction Identification Criteria Matrix

Core Filter Question
"Is the financial position (assets, liabilities, or owner's equity) of the company changed?"
Scenario A: Purchase a Computer ✓ Yes · Record Transaction
Scenario B: Discuss Guided Trip Options with a Potential Customer ✗ No · Do NOT Record
Scenario C: Pay Rent ✓ Yes · Record Transaction

👥 Section 2: Users of Accounting Information

Accounting information serves both those inside the organization (internal users) and outside parties (external users).

💼 Internal Users

Managers who plan, organize, and run the business from the inside.

Finance Departments
"Is cash sufficient to pay dividends to Microsoft stockholders?"
Marketing Staff
"What price should Apple charge for an iPhone to maximize the company's net income?"
Human Resources (HR)
"Can General Motors afford to give its employees pay raises this year?"
Management
"Which PepsiCo product line is the most profitable? Should any product lines be eliminated?"

🌎 External Users

Individuals and organizations outside the company who want financial data about the business.

Investors (Owners / Potential Owners)
"Is General Electric earning satisfactory income?"
"How does Disney compare in size and profitability with WarnerMedia?"
Creditors (Suppliers & Bankers)
"Will United Airlines be able to pay its debts as they come due?"

⚖️ Section 3: Ethics in Financial Reporting

Ethics is a fundamental business concept. For financial reports to be effective, they must be prepared ethically, presenting information that is both relevant and faithfully represented.

1
Recognize an Ethical Situation and the Ethical Issues Involved
Use your personal ethics to identify ethical situations and issues. Note that professional organizations provide written codes of ethics for guidance in business situations.
2
Identify and Analyze the Principal Elements in the Situation
Identify the stakeholders—persons or groups who may be harmed or benefited. Ask the core question: "What are the responsibilities and obligations of the parties involved?"
3
Identify Alternatives, and Weigh the Impact of Each Alternative
Select the most ethical alternative, considering all consequences. Sometimes there will be one right answer; other situations require an evaluation of each and a selection of the best unique alternative.

🧩 Section 4: The Building Blocks & Accounting Equation

The basic components of accounting are assets, liabilities, and owner's equity, bound together mathematically by the accounting equation.

The Basic Accounting Equation

Basic Formula
Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity
Assets
Resources a business owns. These resources represent future economic benefits and values (e.g., Cash, Accounts Receivable, Supplies, Equipment).
Liabilities
Claims against those assets; legal debts and obligations. Creditors represent claims of those to whom the business owes money (e.g., Accounts Payable, Notes Payable).
Owner's Equity
The owner's remaining claim on total assets after all liabilities are deducted. Often referred to as residual equity or net assets.

The Expanded Accounting Equation

Expanded Formula
Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Capital - Owner's Drawings + Revenues - Expenses
▲ Increases Equity

Owner's equity is increased by:

  • Owner's Capital: Assets placed in the business by the owner.
  • Revenues: Gross increases in owner's equity resulting from business activities entered into for the purpose of earning income.

▼ Decreases Equity

Owner's equity is decreased by:

  • Owner's Drawings: Withdrawals of cash or other assets by the owner for personal use.
  • Expenses: The cost of assets consumed or services used in the process of earning revenue.

📋 Section 6: Financial Statements Matrix & Data Timeframes

Companies prepare four financial statements from the summarized accounting data. The timeframe of each statement dictates whether it reports activity over a period or values at a point in time.

1 Income Statement

Reports the success or profitability of the company's operations over a specific period of time.

Timeframe Window: Period of time (e.g., "For the Month Ended October 31, 2022")
Formula: Revenues - Expenses = Net Income / (Net Loss)
2 Owner's Equity Statement

Summarizes the changes in owner's equity that occurred over a specific period of time.

Timeframe Window: Period of time (e.g., "For the Month Ended October 31, 2022")
Formula: Beginning Capital + Investments +/- Net Income/(Loss) - Drawings = Ending Capital
3 Balance Sheet

Reports the assets, liabilities, and owner's equity of a business at a specific point in time.

Timeframe Window: Point in time (e.g., "October 31, 2022")
Formula: Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity (utilizes Ending Capital from Equity Stmt)
4 Statement of Cash Flows

Reports cash receipts, cash payments, and net change in cash resulting from operating, investing, and financing activities.

Timeframe Window: Period of time (e.g., "For the Month Ended October 31, 2022")
Formula: +/- Operating Cash +/- Investing Cash +/- Financing Cash = Net Cash Change + Beg. Cash = Ending Cash

🔗 Section 7: Interconnected Multi-Statement Data Flows

Financial statements are highly interconnected. The output of one statement is a crucial input for the next, forming an integrated reporting loop.

⚡ Flow Step 1: Income Statement to Owner's Equity Statement

The Income Statement calculates the net result of business operations over the period:

Revenues ($4,700) - Expenses ($1,950) = Net Income ($2,750)

Connector Flow: This Net Income of $2,750 is pushed directly into the Owner's Equity Statement as an addition to the beginning capital to calculate changes in equity.

🔄 Flow Step 2: Owner's Equity Statement to Balance Sheet

The Owner's Equity Statement calculates the ending claim of the owner on the assets:

Beg. Capital ($0) + Investments ($15,000) + Net Income ($2,750) - Drawings ($1,300) = Ending Owner's Capital ($16,450)

Connector Flow: This Ending Owner's Capital of $16,450 is pushed directly down into the Owner's Equity section of the Balance Sheet to balance the total Assets.

Alternative Net Loss Scenario: If operational expenses exceed revenues, a Net Loss occurs. For example, if a company has an initial investment of $15,000, incurs a Net Loss of -$10,000, and drawings of -$1,300, it results in a reduced Ending Owner's Capital of $3,700.
💵 Flow Step 3: Statement of Cash Flows to Balance Sheet

The Statement of Cash Flows analyzes how Cash was generated and spent over the period, matching the exact ending Cash balance:

Ending Cash from Cash Flow Statement = $8,050

Connector Flow: The final line item Cash at the end of the period ($8,050) must match the exact Cash Asset ($8,050) reported in the Assets section of the Balance Sheet. This serves as a critical reconciler of cash accounting.

Chapter 2: The Recording Process

The Account, Debit/Credit rules, Journalizing, Posting, Ledger, and Trial Balance

📘 Section 1: The Account and T-Account Mechanics

Understanding the shift from a chronological list to a structured account form.

The Account

An individual accounting record of increases and decreases in a specific asset, liability, or owner's equity item.

The Balancing Rule

Debits must always exceed credits to show a net Debit Balance; Credits must exceed debits to show a net Credit Balance.

The T-Account Standard Layout

In its simplest form, an account consists of three distinct parts:

  1. The Title of the specific account.
  2. A left side, which is strictly called the Debit (Dr.) side.
  3. A right side, which is strictly called the Credit (Cr.) side.

🔄 Section 3: The Three Steps of the Recording Process

The systematic sequence of steps that records economic events chronologically and transfers them to the ledger accounts.

01 Analyze Each Transaction

Determine how the event affects specific accounts using source documents (like an Invoice or receipt).

02 Enter Transaction in a Journal

Record the transaction chronologically in a General Journal to create a complete history of the transaction.

03 Transfer Journal Information to Ledger Accounts

Post the entries by moving the raw debit and credit numbers into the corresponding accounts inside the general ledger.

📝 Section 4: The General Journal & Journalizing Technique

The book of original entry where events are chronologically registered. The recording requires strict syntax and indentation formatting rules.

Syntax Presentation Requirements
  • The date is entered chronologically in the Date Column.
  • The account to be Debited is always listed first and positioned completely flush-left.
  • The account to be Credited is listed on the line directly below and must be explicitly indented to the right.
  • A brief, clear narrative explanation is written on the line directly below the credit entry.
  • A completely blank line must be left between subsequent journal entries to ensure clear visual separation.

🗂️ Section 5: The General Ledger Structure

The collective group of accounts maintained by a business. It contains all assets, liabilities, and owner's equity records organized systematically.

Definition

The entire group of accounts maintained by a business is called the ledger. A general ledger contains all asset, liability, and owner's equity records.

Asset Accounts Stack

Accounts detailing resources owned:

Cash Supplies Prepaid Insurance Equipment Land
Liability Accounts Stack

Accounts tracking obligations and claims:

Notes Payable Accounts Payable Salaries & Wages Payable Interest Payable
Owner's Equity Accounts Stack

Accounts representing residual owner claims:

Owner's Capital Owner's Drawings Service Revenue Salaries & Wages Expense

📂 Section 6: The Posting Process Flow & Entry Pathways

Detailing how transaction data moves dynamically from chronological journal entries into the targeted ledger accounts.

The Posting Definition

The formal procedural transfer of debit and credit entries from the General Journal into the targeted accounts inside the General Ledger.

The Flow Pathways Sequence

1 Step 1 (Date Mapping)
Locate the debit entry line in the General Journal. Post the exact Date and the Debit Amount into the Debit column of the corresponding Ledger Account.
2 Step 2 (Reference Tracking)
In the Ledger Account's Reference (Ref.) column, input 'J1' to link back to General Journal Page 1.
3 Step 3 (Cross-Referencing)
Go back to the General Journal and input the unique Account Number (e.g., 101 for Cash) into the Journal's Reference (Ref.) column.
4 Step 4 (Credit Replication)
Repeat the identical steps for the Credit entry line, sending the Credit Amount down to the credit ledger column and cross-referencing the account number (e.g., 301 for Owner's Capital).

Chapter 3: Adjusting the Accounts

Accrual principles, deferral and accrual adjusting entries, post-adjusted ledger state, and statements flows

📘 Section 1: Timing Issues & Recognition Principles

Accounting relies on precise timing constraints to accurately map operational events to specific calendar periods.

The Time Period Assumption

Accounting divides the economic life of a business into artificial time periods (e.g., months, quarters, or calendar years).

  • Interim Reports: Monthly or quarterly financial statements.
  • Fiscal Year: An accounting time period that is one year in length.
The Recognition Principles

Mapping revenues and expenses to the periods where they truly occur:

  • Revenue Recognition Principle: Companies must recognize revenue in the accounting period in which the performance obligation is satisfied (when the service is performed, NOT when cash is received).
  • Expense Recognition Principle (Matching): Expenses must be matched with revenues in the period when the company makes efforts to generate that revenue ("let the expense follow the revenue").
Accrual vs. Cash Basis

Accrual Basis: complies strictly with GAAP by recording events in the periods they occur. Cash Basis: (recording only when cash moves) violates GAAP.

🔄 Section 2: The Revenue Five-Step Framework

The formal sequence mandated to identify contracts, obligations, transaction prices, and recognition points.

1 Step 1: Identify the contract with customers
Example: Sierra has a contract with the Lewis family to provide guide services.
2 Step 2: Identify the separate performance obligations in the contract
Example: Sierra has only one obligation—to provide guide services.
3 Step 3: Determine the transaction price
Example: The transaction price for Sierra is $1,500.
4 Step 4: Allocate the transaction price to the separate performance obligations
Example: Price allocated entirely to the single guide trip service.
5 Step 5: Recognize revenue when each performance obligation is satisfied
Example: Sierra recognizes $1,500 revenue upon completion of the guide trip.

⏳ Section 3: The Deferral Adjustment Pathways (Prepaids & Unearned)

Deferrals are transactions where cash is exchanged before expense or revenue is recognized.

📈 Deferral Type A: Prepaid Expenses

Prepaid expenses are costs incurred that expire or are consumed through time or operations. Before adjustment, Assets are overstated and Expenses are understated.

Supplies Adjustment Mechanics

Sierra purchased supplies of $2,500. Inventory count on Oct. 31 shows $1,000 left (meaning $1,500 was used).

Basic Analysis: Supplies Expense increases $1,500; the asset Supplies decreases $1,500.
Journal Entry: Debit Supplies Expense (631) $1,500 | Credit Supplies (126) $1,500.
Prepaid Insurance Mechanics

Pioneer paid $600 for a one-year policy on Oct. 4. Expires at a rate of $50 per month ($600 / 12 months).

Journal Entry: Debit Insurance Expense (722) $50 | Credit Prepaid Insurance (130) $50.
Depreciation & Contra Assets

Depreciation is the allocation of the cost of an asset to expense over its useful life. It utilizes a Contra Asset Account to preserve original cost values.

Basic Analysis: Depreciation Expense increases $40; Contra Asset account Accumulated Depreciation—Equipment increases $40.
Journal Entry: Debit Depreciation Expense (711) $40 | Credit Accumulated Depreciation—Equipment (158) $40.

📉 Deferral Type B: Unearned Revenues

Cash is received before the performance obligation is satisfied. Liabilities are overstated and Revenues are understated before adjustments.

Unearned Service Revenue Mechanics

Out of a $1,200 cash advance received on Oct. 2, evaluation shows $400 of service has been performed by Oct. 31.

Basic Analysis: Liability Unearned Service Revenue decreases $400; revenue Service Revenue increases $400.
Journal Entry: Debit Unearned Service Revenue (209) $400 | Credit Service Revenue (400) $400.

📈 Section 4: The Accrual Adjustment Pathways (Revenues & Expenses)

Accruals record transactions that have occurred but are not yet entered or paid in cash.

💵 Accrual Type A: Accrued Revenues

Revenues for services performed but not yet received in cash or recorded. Assets and Revenues are understated before adjustments.

Accrued Revenue Mechanics

Pioneer performed $200 of unbilled, unrecorded services in October.

Basic Analysis: Asset Accounts Receivable increases $200; revenue Service Revenue increases $200.
Journal Entry: Debit Accounts Receivable (112) $200 | Credit Service Revenue (400) $200.

💸 Accrual Type B: Accrued Expenses

Expenses incurred but not yet paid or recorded. Expenses and Liabilities are understated before adjustments.

Accrued Interest Mechanics

Pioneer issued a $5,000 Note Payable at 12% annual interest on Oct. 1. Accrued interest is:
Formula: Face Value x Rate x Time ($5,000 x 12% x 1/12 month = $50).

Journal Entry: Debit Interest Expense (905) $50 | Credit Interest Payable (230) $50.
Accrued Salaries and Wages Mechanics

Pioneer paid two weeks of wages on Oct. 26. Three unpaid working days (Oct. 29, 30, 31) remain in October at a rate of $400/day, totaling $1,200 of accrued salaries.

Journal Entry: Debit Salaries and Wages Expense (726) $1,200 | Credit Salaries and Wages Payable (212) $1,200.
